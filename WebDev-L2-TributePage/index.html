A tribute to

Marie
Curie

7 NOV 1867 — 4 JUL 1934

She discovered two elements, won two Nobel Prizes in two different sciences, and did it all in a language and a country that were not her own — while being told, at every turn, that she couldn't.

"Nothing in life is to be feared, it is only to be understood. Now is the time to understand more, so that we may fear less."

— Marie Curie

What I admire

Persistence

She was refused a place at university in Kraków simply for being a woman, so she studied in secret at Warsaw's underground "Flying University" instead. When Paris finally let her in, she taught herself to live on bread and tea so she could keep affording her classes. Obstacles never seemed to change her direction, only her patience.

Precision

To prove radium existed, she processed tonnes of pitchblende ore by hand in a leaking shed, stirring vats with an iron rod nearly as tall as she was, for years, to isolate a fraction of a gram. That kind of patience with matter, refusing to accept "probably" when "exactly" was possible, is a discipline I find genuinely humbling.

Generosity

She refused to patent the radium isolation process, insisting the discovery belonged to everyone, not to her. During the First World War she personally drove X-ray equipment to the front lines. She gave away most of her prize money. Her instinct, again and again, was to hand the discovery outward rather than hold it close.

The shape of a life

  1. 1891

    Arrives in Paris

    Aged 24, she leaves Warsaw to study physics, chemistry, and mathematics at the Sorbonne, living alone in a Latin Quarter garret.

  2. 1895

    Marries Pierre Curie

    She marries in a plain dark-blue outfit rather than a wedding gown — a dress practical enough to wear in the lab for years afterward.

  3. 1898

    Discovers polonium and radium

    Working from a converted shed, she and Pierre announce two new elements. She names the first after her native Poland.

  4. 1903

    First Nobel Prize

    Shared with Pierre and Henri Becquerel in Physics — she becomes the first woman ever to receive a Nobel Prize.

  5. 1906

    Continues alone

    After Pierre's sudden death, she takes over his chair at the Sorbonne — its first female professor in over 650 years.

  6. 1911

    Second Nobel Prize

    Awarded the Chemistry prize alone, for isolating pure radium — still the only person to win Nobels in two different sciences.

  7. 1914–18

    "Little Curies"

    She designs mobile X-ray units and drives them to the front herself, helping treat over a million wounded soldiers.

  8. 1934

    Passes away

    She dies from aplastic anaemia, almost certainly from decades of unshielded exposure to the very radiation she uncovered.

In the lab

The glow she left behind

Her notebooks are still radioactive. They're kept in lead-lined boxes today, and anyone who wants to read them has to sign a waiver and put on protective clothing first. There's something fitting about that — a century later, her work still asks something of the people who come near it. That's the kind of legacy I hope to leave behind in whatever small way I can: something that still has weight, long after I'm gone.

With admiration,
a fan of radioactive notebooks
